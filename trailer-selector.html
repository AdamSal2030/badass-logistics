The heavy-haul trailers, in plain English

Flatbed

Open 48–53 ft deck (~5 ft high). Legal-height, legal-weight loads that can take the weather — steel, pipe, machinery under ~8'6" tall and ~48,000 lbs.

Flatbed transport →

Step-Deck / Drop-Deck

Lower rear deck (~3'3") buys you height. Machinery up to about 10 ft tall often ships without a height permit.

Step-deck transport →

RGN (Removable Gooseneck)

The nose detaches into a ramp so tall, heavy, self-propelled equipment drives on. Excavators, dozers, cranes; extra axles carry heavier loads.

Lowboy / Double-Drop

Ultra-low well for the tallest loads — up to ~11'6"–12' — and stretch versions for very long freight like beams and blades.

Jeeps and boosters spread superheavy weight across more axles for transformers, vessels, and the heaviest machinery.

Trailer & permit FAQ

How do I know which trailer my load needs?
It comes down to three things: loaded height, weight, and whether the equipment rolls on its own wheels. A load stays legal on a flatbed if it clears 13'6" total height on a ~5 ft deck and weighs under about 48,000 lbs. Taller loads step down to a step-deck; taller or heavier still moves to a double-drop, lowboy, or RGN; and self-propelled machines drive onto an RGN whose gooseneck detaches into a ramp. This tool applies those rules for you.
What makes a load oversize or overweight?
A load is legally oversize once it passes 8'6" wide, 13'6" tall, or roughly 53 ft long, and overweight once the gross combination tops 80,000 lbs. Past those limits you need a state permit; bigger loads add pilot cars, height poles, or bridge analysis. Exact thresholds vary by state, so the permit is confirmed once the route is set.
What is a superload?
A superload is too big or heavy for normal oversize permits — generally over about 16 ft wide, 16 ft tall, 150 ft long, or 200,000 lbs, though each state draws its own line. Superloads usually need engineering review, bridge analysis, police escorts, and route-specific approval.
Is this selector exact?
It's a planning estimate that uses standard U.S. legal limits and typical trailer deck heights to get you to the right category fast. The final trailer, axle setup, and permits are confirmed once we have the exact dimensions, weight, lift points, and route.
