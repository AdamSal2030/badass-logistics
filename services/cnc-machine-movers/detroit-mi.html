Machine-tool moving in the Motor City

Detroit isn't just an auto town — it's the densest concentration of precision machining in North America. The Tier-1 and Tier-2 supplier base that feeds the OEMs runs on CNC: machining centers cutting transmission cases and engine blocks, the legendary tool-and-die shops of Macomb County in Warren and Sterling Heights, the job shops of Livonia, Dearborn, Troy, and Auburn Hills. When one of those machines has to move — a line consolidation, a shop expansion, a new building — it can't be muscled like a pallet. It's a precision instrument that holds ten-thousandths, and it has to come back online holding them.

That's the job Badass Logistics is built for in Metro Detroit. We pull the manufacturer's lift and jacking data before a wrench turns, protect the ways, lock the axes and spindle to their transport positions, and move the machine on air skates and air-ride — then set it, level it, and square it to the builder's spec at the new floor. One accountable crew from disconnect to first cut, and because we run rigging and heavy haul in-house, an oversized machining center gets moved and permitted without a hand-off.

Routes & permits across Metro Detroit

Most CNC moves around Detroit run the I-75, I-94, and I-96 corridors that knit the metro's industrial belt together — Downriver up through Dearborn and Livonia, out to the Macomb shops, and north toward Pontiac and Auburn Hills. A machine that's legal-dimension once crated just gets hauled; a large crated machining center that runs over height or width on those routes needs to be legal on every mile. We handle the Michigan DOT (MDOT) oversize/overweight permitting, route survey, low-bridge and clearance checks, and any escorts as part of the job — so a five-axis cell leaving Sterling Heights gets to its new floor on a compliant route the first time.
